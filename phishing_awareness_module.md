# Phishing Awareness Module

## Introduction

Phishing is a type of cyber attack where criminals try to trick people into revealing sensitive information, such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data. Attackers use email, fake websites, text messages, and social media to impersonate trusted sources.

## How to Recognize Phishing Emails

1. Check the sender address carefully.
   - Look for small differences in the domain name.
   - Example: `support@micros0ft.com` instead of `support@microsoft.com`.
2. Beware of urgent or alarming language.
   - "Your account will be closed," "Immediate action required," or "Payment overdue." 
3. Avoid clicking unexpected attachments or links.
   - Phishing emails often include malicious files or spoofed login pages.
4. Inspect links before clicking.
   - Hover over the link to see the real URL.
   - Example: a link text says `www.bank.com` but the target is `http://malicious.site/login`.
5. Look for spelling and grammar mistakes.
   - Professional organizations rarely send communications with poor language.

## How to Spot Fake Websites

- Check the website address (URL).
  - Secure websites should use `https://` and show a padlock icon.
  - Fake sites may use similar-looking domains or extra words like `login-bank.example.com`.
- Verify the site design and branding.
  - Phishing sites often have low-quality logos or broken layouts.
- Avoid entering sensitive information on pages reached from unsolicited messages.
- Confirm the website through official channels.
  - If unsure, type the known website address manually or use a bookmark.

## Social Engineering Tactics Used by Attackers

1. Pretexting
   - The attacker creates a believable story to gain trust.
   - Example: pretending to be IT support and asking for account credentials.
2. Baiting
   - The attacker offers something tempting, like a free download or gift.
   - Example: "Click here to claim your prize" that leads to malware.
3. Urgency and fear
   - Attackers pressure victims to act quickly before thinking.
   - Example: "Your payment failed and your account will be suspended." 
4. Authority impersonation
   - The attacker poses as a boss, colleague, bank, or government official.
   - Example: an email appearing to come from a manager requesting immediate wire transfer.
5. Familiarity and trust
   - Attackers reference real people or organizations to seem legitimate.
   - Example: using company names, logos, or social media profiles.

## Best Practices to Avoid Phishing

- Never share passwords or sensitive data via email.
- Use strong, unique passwords and enable multi-factor authentication (MFA).
- Verify requests by contacting the company or person directly.
- Keep your software, browser, and operating system updated.
- Use anti-phishing features in email clients and web browsers.
- Report suspicious emails to your IT or security team.

## Real-World Examples

### Example 1: Bank Notification Scam

An email claims your bank account has been locked and asks you to "verify your identity" by logging in through a link. The link goes to a fake site that steals your username and password.

### Example 2: CEO Fraud

An attacker spoofs the CEO's email address and asks an employee to send a confidential file or make a wire transfer immediately. The email looks urgent and official.

### Example 3: Software Update Alert

A message claims your computer needs an urgent security update and includes an attachment. Opening the attachment installs malware instead of updating software.

## Interactive Quiz

### Question 1

You receive an email from your bank asking you to confirm your account information using a link in the message. The sender address is `security@bank-secure.com`. What should you do?

- A) Click the link and enter your details.
- B) Call the bank using the number on your bank statement or official website.
- C) Reply to the email asking if it is legitimate.

**Correct answer:** B

### Question 2

A coworker sends a message asking for a password reset link because they are "locked out". The message seems rushed and the link goes to an unfamiliar URL. What is the safest response?

- A) Send your password.
- B) Open the link to check it.
- C) Confirm with the coworker by phone or messaging app before taking action.

**Correct answer:** C

### Question 3

Which of these is a red flag for a phishing email?

- A) An unexpected attachment from an unknown sender.
- B) A personalized message from your manager using your name.
- C) A link to the company intranet with a valid company domain.

**Correct answer:** A

## Summary

Phishing attacks rely on deception, urgency, and social engineering. By verifying sender details, checking URLs, and practicing safe online habits, you can reduce your risk of falling victim. Always treat unexpected requests for sensitive information with suspicion and confirm before acting.
